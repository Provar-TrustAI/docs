This tutorial continues directly from [Your first 10 minutes in Trust AI](/tutorials/quickstart). You'll set up your first [Evaluators](/concepts/evaluators) — LLM judges that grade sessions against plain-language rules — in two ways: adopting from the recommended catalog, and creating your own with AI-drafted rules. Then you'll **calibrate** the judge against your own pass/fail verdicts. By the end you'll have an Evaluator that agrees with you on the cases you care about.

The whole walkthrough should take about 15 minutes. Most of that time is **iterating on the grading rules**, which is also the most important skill you'll develop as an Evaluator author.

<Tip>
  **You never write a judge prompt.** You state what a passing and a failing response look like as short plain-language rules; Trust AI assembles those rules into the prompt the judge actually runs. The assembled prompt stays visible — read-only — behind an **Advanced** disclosure, so the rules are always the thing you edit.
</Tip>

## Before you start

You should have, from [the quickstart](/tutorials/quickstart):

- A [Project](/concepts/projects) with a connected runtime (or demo data)
- A few [scenarios](/concepts/scenarios) in your Scenarios list — the order-status happy path from the quickstart is perfect
- Your own **pass/fail verdicts** on some of those scenarios: a pass/fail column on the Scenarios list with a few scenarios marked

That last one matters most. Calibration compares the judge's verdicts with yours, so it only works against verdicts you actually recorded. If you haven't marked any scenarios yet, open **Scenarios**, add a pass/fail column, and mark a handful before you start.

## The five steps

<Steps>
  <Step title="Adopt the recommended evaluators">
    <Note>
      *Ask the Trust Agent:* "Add the recommended evaluators to this project."
    </Note>

    Then confirm the result on the Evaluators list; or do it by hand: open **Evaluators** and click **Add recommended** (on a brand-new project, the empty state leads with the same **Add recommended evaluators** call to action).

    The catalog offers 12 curated LLM-judge presets — Quality checks first, then Safety — each with a name, a chip showing where its grading method comes from, and a one-line description. Check the ones that fit your agent and click the footer button, which counts your picks (**Add 3 evaluators**).

    Two things to notice as you pick:

    - A few presets carry a **Tailor first** badge. They're written against a generic agent — add a rule about *your* agent (what it's allowed to promise, what's in scope) before relying on them. The badge is a nudge, not a blocker.
    - Presets already in your project show a muted **In your project** label instead of a checkbox, so you can't adopt duplicates.

    Everything you adopt becomes an ordinary, fully editable Evaluator in your project — it just keeps a **Recommended** chip so its origin stays clear.

    **You should see:** the Evaluators list with your adopted presets, each carrying a **Recommended** chip.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Start your own Evaluator">
    <Note>
      *Ask the Trust Agent:* "Start a new pass/fail evaluator for the order-status flow."
    </Note>

    {/* ACCURACY-AUDIT-PENDING: exact Trust Agent conversational handling of evaluator-create requests not verified at the tag */}
    Or do it by hand: from the Project home, open **Evaluators → New evaluator**.

    The form asks for:

    - **Name** and **Description** — e.g., "Order status — three-part check" with a one-liner on what it's for.
    - **Score type** — **Pass / fail** (a binary verdict per example) or **1–5 scale** (with a **Pass threshold** control: scores at or above the threshold count as a pass). Start with pass/fail; it's the easiest to calibrate.

    Instead of typing grading criteria from scratch, use the **Tailor with AI** box at the top of the form. Describe what the Evaluator should check in a sentence:

    ```
    The agent should identify which order the customer is asking about, give
    that order's actual current status — never a generic "we'll look into it" —
    and explain the next step if the order isn't delivered yet.
    ```

    Click **Draft for my agent**. Draft grading rules appear in the form below as **Passes when…** and **Fails when…** lists, prefilled for you to review and edit. Nothing is saved until you create the Evaluator.

    **You should see:** the New evaluator form with drafted rules under **Passes when…** and **Fails when…**, ready to edit.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Review and tighten the grading rules">
    The rule lists are your rubric. For the order-status flow we curated in the quickstart, a solid set looks like:

    **Passes when…**

    - The agent identifies the order the customer is asking about
    - The agent gives that order's actual current status
    - The agent explains the expected next step when the order isn't delivered yet

    **Fails when…**

    - The agent answers with a generic "we'll check on it" instead of the real status
    - The agent states an order number or status that doesn't match the conversation

    <Tip>
      **The most common rule-writing mistake** is a vague "is this good?" rule. Good rules are testable — each one individually checkable against a single response. If a thoughtful human reviewer couldn't apply your rules and produce the same verdict twice, the LLM judge won't either.
    </Tip>

    You never author the judge prompt itself. Trust AI assembles your rules into the exact criteria the judge runs; on the saved Evaluator you can read the assembled prompt behind **Advanced — view the prompt this produces**, but it's read-only — to change verdicts, you change rules.

    Two options worth knowing before you save:

    - If you picked the **1–5 scale**, the rule lists become three anchors — **Rate 5 when…**, **Rate 3 when…**, **Rate 1 when…** — one sentence each.
    - **Reason step by step** makes the judge work through each rule before deciding. Slower, but better at catching subtle failures; leave it off for your first pass.

    When the rules read right, click **Create evaluator**.

    **You should see:** an "Evaluator created" confirmation, with the new Evaluator's detail panel open over the Evaluators list.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Calibrate against your verdicts">
    <Note>
      *Ask the Trust Agent:* "Check how well my order-status evaluator agrees with my own verdicts."
    </Note>

    Or do it by hand: open your Evaluator and switch to its **Calibrate** tab (LLM judges only). Calibration runs this judge — the same model, rules, and settings production uses — against scenarios you already marked pass or fail, and reports the **agreement**.

    Pick the scenarios to check against and, under **Your verdicts**, the pass/fail column you marked, then click **Calibrate**. The check covers up to 20 marked scenarios and takes about a minute.

    **You should see:** an agreement headline for the latest check, and a **Row by row — disagreements first** table comparing the judge's verdict with yours on each scenario.

    The disagreements are the data — they show exactly where the rules are wrong. Common patterns:

    - The judge passed something you failed → the rules are too lenient. Add a **Fails when…** rule naming that failure.
    - The judge failed something you passed → the rules are too strict. Loosen or remove an over-specified **Passes when…** rule.

    To iterate: edit the rules on the Evaluator's **Criteria** tab and click **Save grading rules**. The Calibrate tab then warns that the criteria changed since the last check — click **Re-calibrate** and watch the agreement move. **Calibration history** keeps every check, newest first, so you can see whether your edits are helping.

    **The goal is roughly 90% agreement** on a small set. Don't chase 100% — perfect agreement on a handful of scenarios usually means the rules over-fit those exact cases.

    Iterate. This is the work.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Put the Evaluator to work">
    <Note>
      *Ask the Trust Agent:* "Attach my order-status evaluator to the order-status scenario."
    </Note>

    Or do it by hand: open the scenario, switch to its **Expected Output** tab, and add your Evaluator with the **+ Add evaluator** picker. The picker lists your project's Evaluators alongside the recommended catalog, with the presets best suited to that scenario surfaced first under **Recommended for this scenario** — adopting one from here attaches it in the same step.

    The Evaluator also lives in your project's Evaluators list, ready for any future [Evaluation](/concepts/evaluations) — it grades scenario runs you launch, and AI judges like this one are also what grade already-captured sessions. It's reusable; that's the whole point. If you'd rather manage Evaluators programmatically, the endpoints are in the [API Reference](/api-reference).

    **You should see:** your Evaluator on the scenario's expected output, next to any structural checks defined there.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## When you don't need a judge at all

If the judgment you're encoding is **exact** — "did the agent route this to order-status?", "did it call the refund tool?" — don't write judge rules for it. Structural checks like expected routes, required or disallowed actions, and parameter checks are authored directly on a scenario's expected output, picked from vocabulary Trust AI learned from your real sessions rather than typed by hand. They're deterministic, they cost nothing per evaluation, and they need no calibration. **If you find yourself encoding something exact into plain-language rules, move it to the scenario's expected output instead.** See [Scenarios](/concepts/scenarios) for how the expected output defines the grading contract.
